In January 2025, wildfires tore through the greater Los Angeles area with a speed and ferocity that overwhelmed one of the most resourced fire departments in the world. The Eaton and Palisades fires alone burned roughly 57,000 acres, destroying nearly 40,000 acres of developed land and claiming over 16,000 structures. Insurance industry losses exceeded $40 billion.

The scale of destruction was staggering. But within that devastation, something stood out, and the wildfire protection lessons are impossible to ignore: homes that had active wildfire protection systems survived at remarkably high rates, even in the hardest-hit neighborhoods. Properties with engineered sprinkler systems (the kind that saturate roofs, eaves, and surrounding areas to prevent ember ignition) remained standing while homes on either side were reduced to ash.

This wasn't luck. It was preparation. And it carries a lesson for every property owner living in a fire-prone area of the United States.

## The Gap Between Fire Response and Fire Reality

The LA fires exposed a truth that wildfire professionals have been warning about for years: when a fire event reaches a certain scale, traditional firefighting resources simply cannot protect every property. LA County Fire Chief Anthony Marrone acknowledged publicly that the departments were not prepared for widespread disaster on that scale.

This isn't a criticism of firefighters. It's a recognition of the mathematical reality. There are more homes in the wildland-urban interface than there are fire crews to defend them. North America faces a 40-50% shortfall in wildfire firefighters, and that gap widens every year as more people move into fire-prone areas and fire seasons grow longer and more intense.

The implication is straightforward: if you're relying solely on emergency services to save your property during a major wildfire event, you may be relying on resources that can't reach you in time.

## The Evidence for Active Protection Is Growing

Among the most important wildfire protection lessons from the LA fires is the compelling evidence for engineered wildfire defence systems. Homes equipped with sprinkler-based protection survived in neighbourhoods where nearly everything else burned.

But this evidence isn't new. In Jasper, Alberta, during the catastrophic 2024 wildfire that destroyed 358 structures, a homeowner who had activated a rooftop sprinkler before evacuating is credited with saving his home and several neighbouring properties. The Jasper fire chief attributed the survival of those homes directly to the sprinkler system keeping the properties saturated.

In Nova Scotia, sprinkler systems deployed on 88 homes during wildfire events saved 85 of them, a success rate that prompted the provincial government to purchase additional units. In northern Saskatchewan, an off-grid cabin survived a wildfire because its owner had installed a system of elevated sprinklers that soaked the property with overlapping coverage.

The pattern is consistent: water-based suppression systems that keep properties saturated are highly effective at preventing the ember ignition that causes 90% of structure loss during wildfires.

## Why Permanent Systems Are Different

After every major fire event, there's a spike in interest in DIY wildfire protection: garden sprinklers on roofs, hoses left running during evacuations, portable pump systems. While the instinct to protect your property is understandable, these approaches carry significant limitations.

Garden sprinklers aren't designed for the sustained, high-coverage application needed during a fire event. They waste water through poor targeting, and if you leave them running during an evacuation, you're potentially reducing the water pressure firefighters need. Portable systems require someone to set them up, and during an evacuation, you need to leave, not be climbing onto your roof.

Permanent, engineered systems address all of these limitations. They're designed for comprehensive coverage of the specific property they're installed on. They use water efficiently, targeting the areas where embers are most likely to ignite. They can be activated remotely, from a phone, from across the country, so you can protect your home while following evacuation orders.

## The Time to Act Is Before Fire Season

These wildfire protection lessons from LA, Jasper, and Nova Scotia all point to the same conclusion: the difference between a home that survives and one that doesn't often comes down to a decision made months or years before the fire arrives.

Defensible space, fire-resistant construction, and community preparedness are all important layers of protection. But they work best when combined with an active defence system that addresses the primary threat (ember attack) head on.
